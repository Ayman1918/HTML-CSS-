Central Karakoram National Park - Skardu Valley

Best time to visit Central Karakoram National Parks

The best time to visit the Central Karakoram National Park is usually between May and September, when the weather is generally moderate and there is usually little snowfall. This period also provides the best opportunities for trekking, as most trails are accessible and not blocked by snow. Wildlife watchers can also take advantage of the warm weather to spot animals more easily.

ANIMALS

In this region, you can find a variety of wildlife, including:

1. Snow Leopard: A rare and endangered big cat that lives in the high mountains of the Karakoram Range.

2. Himalayan Ibex: A type of wild goat that lives in the rocky terrain of the Karakoram Mountains.

3. Marco Polo Sheep: A large species of wild sheep that inhabits the high mountains of Central Asia, including the Karakoram Range.

4. Asiatic Black Bear: A species of bear that lives in the forested areas of the Karakoram Mountains.

5. Himalayan Marmot: A large ground squirrel that lives in the alpine meadows of the Karakoram Range.

6. Golden Eagle: A large bird of prey that can be found in the Karakoram Mountains.

7. Tibetan Wolf: A subspecies of the gray wolf that inhabits the high altitudes of the Karakoram Mountains.

BIRDS

In this region, you can find a variety of wildlife, including:

1.Golden Eagle: This large bird of prey is found throughout the Karakoram Range and is known for its impressive hunting skills.

2. Himalayan Snowcock: This large, ground-dwelling bird can be found in the high-altitude meadows of the Karakoram Mountains.

3. Himalayan Monal: This colorful bird is found in the forests and alpine meadows of the Karakoram Range and is the national bird of Pakistan..

4. Lammergeier: Also known as the bearded vulture, this bird is found in the high mountains of the Karakoram Range and is known for its scavenging habits.

5. Wallcreeper: This small, colorful bird is found in the rocky cliffs and gorges of the Karakoram Mountains..

6. White-capped Water Redstart: This small bird is found along the rivers and streams of the Karakoram Range and is known for its distinctive white cap..

Pakistan: Too Risky or Safe Enough?- Skardu Valley

A helicopter flies over a vast glacier in Pakistan's Karakorum.

Pakistan’s Karakorum is home to 108 peaks above 7,000m and four above 8,000m: K2, Gasherbrum I, Gasherbrum II and Broad Peak. (The country’s fifth 8,000’er, Nanga Parbat, is part of the Himalayan range.) Despite these riches, Pakistan remains much less visited than the Himalaya. Even K2, the second highest rock on earth, looms comparatively untouched. So what’s holding Pakistan back from a similar level of commercial success.

Those international visitors who do come to Pakistan experience a very different country from the black domain of kidnappings, terrorist attacks and general hostility presented by western embassies. They describe a huge, vibrant country of stunning natural beauty, and a people that greet travellers with warmth and generosity. So which is it: Is Pakistan a too high a risk for foreign tourists to run? Or is it the victim of an international community that focuses unduly on its problems

Famed adventurer Ned Gillette was killed by gunmen in northern Kashmir in 1998. Photo: Adventure-journal.com

It is hard to deny that when Pakistan makes the headlines, it’s rarely positive news. More typically, coverage focuses on drone strikes, terrorism and corruption. Its government has repeatedly been castigated for its unwillingness, or inability, to stem terrorism. Its porous border with Afghanistan remains a stronghold for a hodgepodge of terrorist groups, from traditional baddies such as the Taliban and Al-Qaeda to more obscure but no less hazardous entities such as Lashkar-e-Tayyiba and Jaish-e-Mohammed, operating out of Pakastani-administered Kashmir.
